About Me

Amazon One Medical QI Program Manager Cervical Cancer Screening Population Health Evaluation

For my Applied Practice Experience, I am completing work at my employer, Amazon One Medical, where I serve as a Quality Improvement Program Manager in a population health and quality improvement setting. My APE focuses on supporting preventive care through the development of cervical cancer screening awareness campaigns and a population health evaluation framework.

This experience connects directly to public health practice because cervical cancer screening is a preventive health measure that can reduce morbidity and mortality when patients are screened appropriately and follow-up care is completed. At the population health level, this work involves thinking beyond individual visits and looking at how systems can identify care gaps, communicate effectively with patients, support clinical teams, and evaluate whether outreach efforts are making a meaningful impact.

Subsection

Population Health Evaluation Framework

A major part of my APE involves thinking through how a cervical cancer screening awareness campaign can be evaluated. In population health, it is not enough to simply create an intervention. It is also important to understand whether the intervention reaches the intended audience, supports patient action, and helps care teams identify areas for improvement.

This evaluation framework helps connect campaign planning with measurable outcomes. It may include process measures such as outreach completion, engagement with educational materials, and care gap identification, as well as outcome-oriented measures related to screening completion and follow-up. This part of the experience is helping me better understand how evaluation can make public health work more practical, accountable, and meaningful.

Reach

Proportion of eligible patients who received outreach materials or communications about cervical cancer screening.

Engagement

Patient interaction with educational resources, appointment scheduling, and care team follow-up prompts.

Screening Completion

Rate of eligible patients completing cervical cancer screening after outreach, compared to baseline.

Follow-Up & Improvement

Identification of persistent care gaps and opportunities to refine campaign design for future cycles.

Learning

Reflection

This Applied Practice Experience is helping me connect classroom concepts to real-world population health work. In public health courses, we often discuss prevention, evaluation, equity, and systems thinking. Through this project, I am seeing how those ideas show up in practical ways — such as designing outreach, supporting screening campaigns, and thinking carefully about how success should be measured.

One important takeaway so far is that improving preventive care is not only about reminding patients to complete screenings. It also requires clear communication, reliable data, thoughtful workflows, and an understanding of the barriers that may prevent people from getting care. This experience is helping me develop a more practical understanding of how population health strategies can be designed, implemented, and evaluated within a healthcare organization.

I am also gaining a deeper appreciation for the intersection of equity and quality improvement. Campaigns that look effective on aggregate may still leave specific populations behind. Designing outreach that is both measurable and equitable is one of the most meaningful challenges I have encountered through this experience — and one I expect to carry forward in my public health career.

Population health work is strongest when data, communication, and patient-centered design come together.

Key Takeaways

  • Preventive care improvement requires systems-level thinking, not just individual reminders
  • Evaluation transforms a campaign from a one-time effort into an ongoing learning cycle
  • Data is most powerful when paired with equitable, patient-centered communication
  • Academic concepts come alive when applied in real healthcare workflows
